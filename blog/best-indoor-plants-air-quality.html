Best Indoor Plants for Air Quality and Energy

Updated February 2026  ·  18 min read  ·  stimulant.homes

Indoor air quality directly affects your energy, focus, and overall health. The average person spends approximately 90% of their time indoors, where air pollutant concentrations can be two to five times higher than outdoor levels according to the EPA. The right houseplants act as natural air purifiers, removing volatile organic compounds while producing fresh oxygen. This guide covers the best air-purifying indoor plants backed by NASA research, how many you actually need, where to place them for maximum benefit, and complete care instructions for every skill level.

The Science Behind Plants and Air Quality

NASA's Clean Air Study, conducted in 1989 by Dr. B.C. Wolverton, tested the ability of common houseplants to remove volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from sealed chambers. VOCs are chemicals emitted by everyday items including furniture, paint, cleaning products, printers, and building materials. The most common indoor VOCs are formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene, and ammonia.

The study found that certain plants could remove up to 87% of air toxins within 24 hours in a sealed environment. Plants absorb these gases through their leaves and roots, where microorganisms in the soil break down the chemicals into harmless compounds. The plants then release clean oxygen as a byproduct of photosynthesis.

Beyond chemical filtration, plants increase relative humidity through transpiration. This is particularly beneficial during winter months when indoor heating systems dry out the air, causing dry skin, irritated eyes, and increased susceptibility to respiratory infections. A 2019 study in the Journal of Environmental Psychology found that the mere presence of indoor plants reduced perceived stress by 37% and improved task performance by 12% in office environments.

The energy-boosting effects of indoor plants come from multiple mechanisms: increased oxygen levels improve cognitive function, reduced VOC exposure eliminates the fatigue caused by low-level chemical exposure (sometimes called sick building syndrome), and the psychological calming effect of greenery reduces the cortisol that causes energy crashes throughout the day.

Top 10 Air Purifying Indoor Plants

1. Snake Plant (Sansevieria trifasciata)

Best Overall
Snake Plant - The Bedroom Champion
The snake plant is the most recommended air purifying plant for good reason. It is one of the few plants that converts CO2 to oxygen at night through Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM photosynthesis), making it ideal for bedrooms. NASA's study ranked it among the top plants for removing formaldehyde, benzene, xylene, and toluene. It tolerates low light, irregular watering (every 2-4 weeks), and extreme neglect. Virtually impossible to kill. Available in dozens of varieties from 6-inch tabletop versions to 4-foot floor plants. Price: $8-25 depending on size. One of the best investments you can make for your indoor environment.

2. Pothos (Epipremnum aureum)

Easiest to Grow
Pothos - The Starter Plant
Pothos is arguably the easiest houseplant to grow and one of the most effective at removing formaldehyde, which is the most common indoor VOC released by furniture, flooring, and household products. It grows in almost any light condition, from bright indirect to low light. Water when the top inch of soil is dry, roughly once per week. The trailing vines grow quickly (6-12 inches per month in good conditions) and can be trained on shelves, hung in baskets, or grown in water without soil. Propagation is effortless: cut a section with a node and place it in water. Price: $5-15. Toxic to pets if ingested, so keep on high shelves if you have cats or dogs.

3. Peace Lily (Spathiphyllum)

Top NASA Pick
Peace Lily - The VOC Destroyer
The peace lily scored highest in NASA's study for removing the widest range of VOCs: formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene, ammonia, and toluene. It is one of the few plants effective against ammonia, making it excellent for kitchens and bathrooms. Peace lilies also increase humidity by up to 5% through transpiration, which helps prevent dry skin and respiratory issues during winter. They produce elegant white flowers several times per year. Moderate light preference but tolerates low light (fewer flowers). Water when leaves start to droop slightly. Price: $10-30. Mildly toxic to pets.

4. Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum)

Best for Beginners
Spider Plant - The Prolific Producer
Spider plants are exceptionally effective at removing formaldehyde and carbon monoxide from indoor air. NASA's tests showed spider plants removed 95% of formaldehyde from a sealed chamber. They are non-toxic to pets, making them the top choice for households with animals. Spider plants produce "pups" (baby plants) prolifically, so one plant quickly becomes many at no additional cost. They thrive in indirect light and prefer to dry out between waterings. Brown tips usually indicate chlorine in tap water; use filtered water if this occurs. Price: $5-12. One of the most forgiving plants available.

5. Rubber Plant (Ficus elastica)

Best Statement Plant
Rubber Plant - The Formaldehyde Fighter
Rubber plants are particularly effective at removing formaldehyde from indoor air. Their large, thick leaves have significant surface area for gas absorption and oxygen production. They grow into impressive floor plants reaching 6-10 feet indoors with proper care, making a strong visual statement while purifying your air. Prefer bright indirect light but tolerate medium light. Water when the top 2 inches of soil are dry. Wipe leaves monthly with a damp cloth to remove dust and maintain their air-filtering efficiency. Price: $15-40. The Burgundy variety has dramatic dark red-black leaves.

6. Boston Fern (Nephrolepis exaltata)

Best Humidifier
Boston Fern - Natural Humidity Machine
Boston ferns are the champion of natural humidity. They transpire more moisture than almost any other houseplant, effectively acting as a natural humidifier. This makes them excellent for combating dry indoor air, particularly during winter heating seasons. They scored highly in NASA's study for removing formaldehyde and xylene. Boston ferns prefer bright indirect light and consistently moist (not soggy) soil. They drop leaves when the air is too dry, so misting helps. Hanging baskets in bathrooms are their ideal placement. Pet-safe. Price: $10-20. Higher maintenance than snake plants or pothos, but the humidity benefits are unmatched.

7. Areca Palm (Dypsis lutescens)

Best Large Plant
Areca Palm - The Living Air Purifier
Dr. Wolverton rated the areca palm as the single best air purifying plant overall. It excels at removing formaldehyde, xylene, and toluene. A 6-foot areca palm transpires approximately 1 liter of water per 24 hours, making it a powerful natural humidifier. These palms are non-toxic to pets and children. They prefer bright indirect light and consistent watering when the top inch of soil dries. They grow 6-12 inches per year indoors. One mature areca palm can effectively filter the air in a 100 square foot room. Price: $20-60 depending on size. The best investment for a single large air purifying plant.

8. ZZ Plant (Zamioculcas zamiifolia)

Most Drought Tolerant
ZZ Plant - The Survivor
The ZZ plant is nearly impossible to kill. It stores water in its thick rhizomes and can survive months without watering. It thrives in low light conditions that would kill most plants, including windowless rooms with only fluorescent lighting. Studies from the University of Copenhagen (2014) found ZZ plants effective at removing xylene, toluene, and benzene from indoor air. The glossy dark green leaves are naturally shiny and do not collect dust as readily as other plants. Water every 3-4 weeks or when the soil is completely dry. Price: $12-30. The Raven variety has stunning near-black foliage. Toxic to pets if ingested.

9. Dracaena (multiple varieties)

Most Versatile
Dracaena - The Variety King
The Dracaena genus includes dozens of species, many of which appeared in NASA's Clean Air Study. Dracaena marginata (dragon tree), Dracaena fragrans (corn plant), and Dracaena deremensis (Janet Craig) are all highly effective at removing formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, and xylene. They come in sizes from tabletop to 6-foot floor plants. Most varieties tolerate low to medium light and prefer to dry out between waterings. Their narrow, arching leaves give them an architectural quality that works in modern spaces. Price: $10-50. Toxic to pets.

10. English Ivy (Hedera helix)

Best for Allergies
English Ivy - The Mold Fighter
English ivy is unique among air purifying plants because of its ability to reduce airborne mold. A 2005 study found that English ivy reduced airborne mold by 78% within 12 hours. It also filters formaldehyde, benzene, and trichloroethylene effectively. This makes it particularly valuable for people with allergies or asthma. English ivy grows as a trailing vine, perfect for hanging baskets or training on a small trellis. It prefers bright indirect light and consistent moisture. It can be grown outdoors but is considered invasive in some regions, so keep it indoors. Price: $5-15. Toxic to pets.

Best Plants for Your Bedroom

Bedroom plants should ideally produce oxygen at night, not just during the day. Most plants only photosynthesize during daylight hours, but CAM plants continue the process at night. The best bedroom plants are also quiet growers that will not drop leaves or require frequent maintenance.

  • Snake plant: Produces oxygen at night. Requires almost zero care. Does not attract pests. The single best bedroom plant.
  • Aloe vera: Another CAM plant that produces oxygen at night. Also useful for treating minor burns and skin irritations. Prefers bright indirect light.
  • Lavender: While not a traditional air purifier, lavender's scent has been clinically shown to reduce heart rate and blood pressure, promoting better sleep quality. Needs bright light and good air circulation.
  • Peace lily: Increases bedroom humidity, which prevents the dry throat and nasal passages that disrupt sleep during winter months.

Place 2-3 plants in your bedroom for optimal benefit. Position one snake plant within 6 feet of your bed for nighttime oxygen production, and one peace lily or aloe vera on a dresser or nightstand for additional air quality improvement.

Best Plants for Your Home Office

Home office environments typically have higher concentrations of VOCs from electronics, printers, furniture off-gassing, and synthetic materials. The right plants can reduce the headaches, fatigue, and difficulty concentrating associated with poor office air quality.

  • Pothos: Place on a bookshelf or desk edge. Minimal care required and thrives in typical office lighting. Removes formaldehyde from furniture and flooring.
  • Snake plant: Place in a corner. Zero maintenance distraction during work hours. Tall varieties add visual interest without taking desk space.
  • Rubber plant: A single large rubber plant acts as both air purifier and natural room divider. Its large leaves are highly efficient at gas exchange.
  • Spider plant: Excellent for desk placement. Removes formaldehyde and carbon monoxide. Non-toxic if you have children who might touch it.

Studies from the Human Spaces Report (2015) found that employees with plants in their workspace reported 15% higher levels of creativity and 6% higher productivity compared to those without plants. For a home office, aim for at least 2 plants: one on or near your desk and one larger plant on the floor.

Best Air Purifying Plants for Low Light

Not every room has bright windows. Bathrooms, hallways, basement offices, and north-facing rooms often receive minimal natural light. These plants thrive in low-light conditions while still providing air purification benefits.

  • ZZ plant: Survives in near-darkness including rooms with only fluorescent lighting.
  • Snake plant: Tolerates low light although growth slows significantly. Will not die from low light conditions.
  • Pothos: Adapts to low light by producing darker green leaves. Growth slows but the plant remains healthy.
  • Cast iron plant (Aspidistra elatior): Named for its ability to survive almost any condition. Extremely low light tolerant. Removes formaldehyde. Nearly impossible to kill.
  • Chinese evergreen (Aglaonema): Thrives in low to medium light. Multiple colorful varieties available. Removes formaldehyde and benzene. Water when soil is dry.

Pet-Safe Air Purifying Plants

If you have cats or dogs, plant toxicity is a serious consideration. Many of the most effective air purifiers (pothos, peace lily, philodendron) are toxic to pets. Fortunately, several excellent pet-safe alternatives exist.

  • Spider plant: Non-toxic to cats and dogs. Highly effective air purifier. Easy to grow.
  • Boston fern: Completely pet-safe. Excellent humidity booster and air purifier.
  • Areca palm: Non-toxic. One of the best overall air purifiers.
  • Parlor palm (Chamaedorea elegans): Non-toxic. Tolerates low light. Compact growth habit.
  • Calathea: Non-toxic. Beautiful patterned leaves. Moderate care requirements. Removes formaldehyde.
  • Money tree (Pachira aquatica): Non-toxic. Interesting braided trunk. Easy care.

Placement Strategy for Maximum Benefit

Strategic placement matters more than total plant count. Focus your plants where you spend the most time and where air quality issues are highest.

  • Bedroom (8+ hours/day): 2-3 plants. Prioritize snake plant for nighttime oxygen. Place within 6 feet of where you sleep.
  • Home office (6-8 hours/day): 2-4 plants. One desk plant, one floor plant. Focus on formaldehyde removers near furniture and electronics.
  • Living room: 3-5 plants depending on size. One large floor plant (areca palm, rubber plant) plus smaller accent plants. Group plants together for visual impact and a humidity microclimate.
  • Kitchen: Peace lily for ammonia removal. Herbs like basil and mint for dual purpose (air quality plus cooking). Place away from direct heat sources.
  • Bathroom: Boston fern or peace lily thrive in high-humidity environments. Place in bright bathrooms with windows for best results.
  • Entryway/Hallway: ZZ plant or snake plant for low-light conditions. These areas often have the most outdoor pollutants tracked in.

The NASA recommendation is one medium-sized plant per 100 square feet. For a 1,500 square foot home, that is approximately 15 plants. Start with 5-6 in your highest-priority rooms and add more over time. Even 3-5 plants provide measurable air quality and psychological benefits.

Care Guide for Beginners

Overwatering kills more houseplants than any other cause. The single most important rule is to check the soil moisture before watering. Stick your finger 1-2 inches into the soil. If it feels dry, water. If it feels moist, wait. This simple check prevents 90% of houseplant deaths.

  • Light: Most air-purifying plants prefer bright indirect light (near a window but not in direct sun). Direct sunlight through a window can burn leaves. Rotate plants quarterly for even growth.
  • Water: Use room-temperature water. Water until it drains from the bottom of the pot. Empty the drainage tray after 30 minutes to prevent root rot. Most plants need water every 7-14 days depending on season, humidity, and light.
  • Soil: Use well-draining potting mix. Add perlite (20-30%) to standard potting soil for better drainage. Never use garden soil in pots.
  • Pots: Always use pots with drainage holes. Terracotta pots are ideal because they are porous and prevent overwatering. Ceramic and plastic pots retain more moisture.
  • Fertilizer: Feed monthly during spring and summer with a balanced liquid fertilizer diluted to half strength. Do not fertilize in winter when growth slows.
  • Cleaning: Wipe leaves monthly with a damp cloth. Dust blocks the stomata (leaf pores) that absorb air pollutants and exchange gases. Clean leaves are more effective air purifiers.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1
Overwatering
More plants die from overwatering than underwatering. Yellowing lower leaves, mushy stems, and a musty smell are signs of overwatering. Root rot is usually fatal. Always check soil moisture before watering and ensure pots have drainage holes. When in doubt, wait another 2-3 days before watering.
Mistake #2
Placing plants in direct sunlight
Most air-purifying plants evolved in tropical forest understories where they receive filtered light. Direct sunlight through a window, especially south-facing windows, causes leaf scorching and bleaching. Move plants 2-5 feet from windows or use sheer curtains to filter intense light. Only succulents and cacti prefer direct sun.
Mistake #3
Ignoring dust buildup on leaves
Dusty leaves cannot effectively filter air pollutants or photosynthesize efficiently. Monthly leaf cleaning with a damp cloth or a gentle shower restores their air purifying capacity. This is particularly important for large-leaved plants like rubber plants and peace lilies. Think of it as maintaining a filter: a clogged filter does not work.
Mistake #4
Buying too many plants at once
Start with 2-3 easy plants (snake plant, pothos, ZZ plant) and learn their care rhythms before adding more. Every home has different light, humidity, and temperature conditions. What works in one house may struggle in another. Master the basics first, then expand your collection gradually.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which indoor plants produce the most oxygen?
Snake plants are among the highest oxygen producers and uniquely continue producing oxygen at night through CAM photosynthesis. Areca palms produce significant daytime oxygen and were rated by NASA as one of the best air purifiers. Pothos and spider plants also produce substantial oxygen. Place 2-3 medium plants per 100 square feet for maximum oxygen benefit.
Do indoor plants actually improve air quality?
Yes. NASA's Clean Air Study proved certain plants remove VOCs like formaldehyde, benzene, and trichloroethylene. In real homes (not sealed chambers), the purification effect is more modest, but studies confirm indoor plants reduce physiological stress, lower blood pressure, and improve comfort. The combined physical and psychological benefits are well-documented in peer-reviewed research.
What are the lowest maintenance air purifying plants?
Snake plants tolerate low light, irregular watering (every 2-4 weeks), and neglect. ZZ plants survive months without water and thrive in low light. Pothos grows in almost any condition. Cast iron plants tolerate low light, temperature changes, and drought. All four are excellent for beginners and busy people.
How many plants do I need to purify the air in my home?
NASA recommends one plant per 100 square feet. For a 1,000 square foot apartment, that is about 10 medium plants. However, even 3-5 plants in the rooms where you spend the most time (bedroom, office, living room) will provide noticeable air quality improvement and psychological benefits.
Are air purifying plants safe for pets?
Some are toxic to cats and dogs. Peace lilies, pothos, and philodendrons can cause oral irritation. Pet-safe alternatives include spider plants, Boston ferns, areca palms, calatheas, and parlor palms. The ASPCA maintains a comprehensive toxicity database. Choose pet-safe varieties or keep toxic plants completely out of reach.
